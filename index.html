Part 1 — Practicing with generative AI

The same prompt — marketing ideas for a Carrollton, TX cleaning service — was run three separate times through Claude to compare outputs, rather than treating the first answer as final. Each run surfaced a different lever: trust-building (background checks, guarantees, referrals), seasonal/local timing (allergy season, HOA flyers, back-to-school), and social proof (before/after photos, a "meet your cleaner" series).

The same thread was then pushed further with a custom GPT, using follow-up moves from the lab: asking it to revise for a tighter budget, adding the constraint of a solo-operator business, asking for sharper copy, asking it to justify its own prioritization, exploring a "what if we expand to a neighboring city" scenario, and finally asking for a summary — which held context well across every turn.

Finally, STORM AI was used as a research assistant on a related topic: how AI-assisted scheduling tools affect small service businesses. Rather than answering in one pass, it generated sub-questions and simulated perspectives first, then used those to guide research before assembling a structured, cited article — closer to how a person would outline before drafting.

Part 2 — Problem-solving reflection

The lab also asked for a reflection on how problems get approached in general. The starting point is understanding a problem fully before reacting — close to the idea of tabayyun, verifying before acting, since a solution built on a misread problem rarely holds up. Judging whether something actually worked means looking at real outcomes and honest feedback rather than how smooth the process felt, which ties to muhasabah — regularly re-checking your own work instead of assuming it's correct.

User-centric thinking follows the same logic: picturing the constraints — time, budget, stress — of the person facing the problem before designing anything, in the spirit of ihsan, doing the work with the same care as if the outcome mattered personally. Empathy is treated as the reason Design Thinking starts where it does — without it, it's easy to solve the wrong problem well.

Part 3 — Designing "Social Spark"

The build ran the full Design Thinking cycle for a chatbot aimed at small business owners who are time-poor, budget-constrained, and not social-media specialists — and don't have the time or budget to keep up with trends or hire outside help.

Problem statement: small business owners need a fast, free way to get social post ideas that feel authentic to their audience and region, without researching trends themselves.

Concept: given a business type, audience, and region, the bot returns a short list of post ideas — hook, caption, and format — without re-asking for the same details every conversation.

ObjectiveGenerate relatable, on-trend social post ideas tailored to a small business's audience and region.
Knowledge sourceGeneral social media best practice plus whatever the user shares about their business — no live trend-scraping.
Explicit boundariesNever claims to guarantee revenue or results, write full ad campaigns, or give financial/legal advice.
Interaction styleOne combined intake question, then 3–5 ideas — not three separate questions.
Test scenario"A coffee shop in Austin, TX targeting young professionals" — checked that ideas were specific, not generic.

The build tool was Chatbase's free tier, with a system prompt carrying explicit memory rules so the bot never re-asks for information it already has, plus a reference knowledge base (format guides, hook formulas, seasonal prompts) to sharpen specificity. Branding — logo, headline "Got 2 minutes? Let's find your next post," and quick-start buttons — was added for a clear first-time experience.

Reflection

The most useful single decision was setting explicit memory rules in the system prompt — without them, the bot would re-ask basic questions and undercut the entire "efficient for busy owners" premise the project was built around. Testing the same prompt multiple times in Part 1 and running an empathy-first framework in Parts 2–3 kept the bot's scope honest: focused on saving time rather than overpromising results it can't deliver.

03 — Graduate Reflection

Personal Assessment — Leading Change

AIML-500

A self-assessment comparing strengths and gaps across two instruments — AI/ML Integration Leadership and Change Management — written for the Machine Learning Fundamentals course's leading-change module.

Leadership Change Management Self-Assessment
Read the full reflection

AI/ML integration leadership

The top three strengths identified were technology awareness, strategic thinking, and collaboration — a grasp of how AI and ML can meet organizational objectives, an emphasis on purposeful rather than symbolic implementation, and communication skills that support cross-team rollout. The areas flagged for growth were a deeper grounding in AI/ML concepts themselves, the ethical and risk-management side of AI, and turning strategy into concrete implementation plans.

Change management

The strongest areas here were communication, stakeholder engagement, and adaptability — effective communication lowers uncertainty and helps people embrace change, stakeholder engagement builds the trust a change program depends on, and adaptability supports problem-solving when plans shift. The growth areas were measuring the impact of change, handling resistance, and building coaching skills to help others adapt.

Evaluation and recommendations

Both instruments were useful for self-reflection but are still self-report, so pairing them with peer and manager feedback would round out the picture. The AI/ML assessment would benefit from added questions on governance, data privacy, and measuring the business value of AI; the change management assessment from more on emotional intelligence, employee engagement, and formal change frameworks. Together, the two are treated as a starting point rather than a verdict — a way to point toward the leadership skills needed for AI/ML integration and organizational change, not a finished score.
